Moving to Ann Arbor from another country adds a few wrinkles to an already stressful
apartment search: no U.S. credit history, a compressed timeline, and signing a lease for
a place you've never seen. This guide collects what current students wish they'd known.

## Start with the timeline

Ann Arbor's rental market is competitive and moves **earlier than you might expect**.
Many 12-month leases for a fall start are signed the previous winter or spring. If you're
admitted in spring, it's reasonable to start looking right away.

If you can't sign a full lease before arriving, a common strategy is to:

1. Line up a **short-term sublet** for your first weeks or first term, then
2. Sign a longer lease once you're on the ground and can view places in person.

Summer sublets are often available from students leaving for internships.

## Where students look

- **U-M Off-Campus Housing / Beyond the Diag**: the university's off-campus housing
  program and listings, run through the [Dean of Students office](https://deanofstudents.umich.edu/).
- **U-M Housing**: university-managed options including graduate and family housing such as
  Northwood (on North Campus) and the Munger Graduate Residences. See
  [housing.umich.edu](https://housing.umich.edu/).
- **Student and departmental groups**: CSEG and other grad channels often have people
  looking for roommates or advertising sublets. Ask in the CSEG Slack (request an invite
  from the [Contact page]({{ '/contact/' | relative_url }})).

## Neighborhoods, roughly

Ann Arbor is small, but where you live changes your daily commute and vibe:

- **North Campus**: closest to CSE (the Bob & Betty Beyster Building is here). Quieter, more
  grad students and families, and home to Northwood's university apartments.
- **Central Campus / Downtown**: livelier and walkable to restaurants and nightlife, but
  busier with undergrads and often pricier.
- **Areas a bit further out**: can be more affordable; check the bus connections before you commit.

U-M runs free campus buses between North and Central Campus, and the city's
[TheRide](https://www.theride.org/) buses are free for U-M students with an MCard, so you
don't necessarily need a car.

## The international-student hurdles

These are the parts that catch people from abroad off guard:

- **No U.S. credit history.** Many landlords run a credit check. Without one, you may be asked
  for a larger deposit, several months' rent up front, or a U.S.-based guarantor. Ask the
  landlord what they accept *before* you fall in love with a place.
- **Proof of funding / status.** Some landlords accept an admission or funding letter in place
  of a credit history. The [U-M International Center](https://internationalcenter.umich.edu/)
  can help with documentation and questions about your status.
- **SSN and bank account come *after* you arrive.** You generally can't get a Social Security
  Number until you're in the U.S. and have reported to the International Center, so plan to
  handle some setup (bank account, deposits) in your first weeks.
- **Timing your move-in with your visa and travel.** Don't sign a lease that starts before you
  can realistically arrive; build in buffer.

## Questions worth asking a landlord

- What's required to apply without U.S. credit history?
- What's included in rent (heat, water, internet, parking)?
- What are the total move-in costs (deposit + first/last month)?
- How is subletting handled if you leave for a summer internship?
